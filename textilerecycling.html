My major individual project Fab! recycles unwanted high-quality postconsumer clothing into attractive and varied pieces of patchwork fabric, for diverse end applications. By doing so it aims to mitigate the numerous impacts resulting from the global consumption of clothing, and to raise awareness of the problem.

A short essay summarising my concerns on the topic can be found here.

My first piece of work studied the environmental problems resulting from clothing consumption. I found that the production of cotton clothing is energy, water and chemical intense, with broader ecological and biodiversity concerns stemming from farming practices.
My study also found that clothing is short-lived in the UK, with an average use phase of 3.3 years. The disposal of unwanted clothing creates another huge challenge, with an uncomfortable quantity being landfilled or incinerated.
This research confirmed my project's direction as a recycling initiative, as by producing new products from reclaimed material impacts incurred at production and disposal would be avoided.

After conducting a group brainstorm to identify and segment as many applications for textiles as possible, I carried out a social media study to confirm which items are most frequently produced using patchwork.
I then identified uses with high potential for the project using a 2D criteria map. The items which were eventually selected were tote bags, technology cases and pencil cases, shopping bags, mats/ rugs and quilts.
My second investigative report researched the academic and industrial attempts at automated sewing. The research was split into areas of sensing techniques, handling mechanisms, material properties and control methods. It was found that modern advancements in computing have enabled more sophisticated vision and control techniques. Most research aims towards automated garment manufacture, but the project differs from this challenge in that only linear sewn profiles are required.
Left: Brother Vision Sewing System Right: Vetron AutoSeam prototype

I conducted a series of experiments to broadly categorise the material properties of different fabrics likely to be encountered. A tensile test rig was built to support the experiments.
The experiments observed highly non-linear and anisotrophic material properties, particularly in knitted fabrics. This data and understanding will be used during prototyping to maintain applied forces to appropriate levels.
Left: Knitted sample, showing non-linear and anisotropic behaviour

Right: Woven sample, showing linear and isotropic behaviour

Design development of the system was informed by these research reports, and after brainstorming key decisions were made regarding stitch type and manufacturing layout were made.
The overlock sewing process was selected to give produced patchwork the best quality finish. A 'workbench' manufacturing strategy, where the fabric would be produced in situ, was originally considered, however a 'cell' based strategy, where operations would be conducted separately, was selected to reduce complexity and increase cycle time.

Alternative options were considered for manipulation, sensing and control and due to the system complexity a morphological chart was used to identify different concepts.
To investigate the feasibility of manipulating fabric using suction, a low-fidelity prototype was made. Powered by a domestic vacuum, this performed unexpectedly well, and offered confidence that patches could be lifted if required.
Different manipulations options were explored to support actions required of the system.
I experimented with machine vision and created a program which could reliably recognise a square fabric patch and identify its centre and angle. This will be used to ensure the machine is highly automated and reliable, carrying out pick-and-place operations accurately.
The following iterative design, prototyping, testing and development work will aim to follow a process of increasing automation, and then increasing unification of stages. In this way the complexity of the system will be broken into discrete challenges.
