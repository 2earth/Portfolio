The team were tasked with designing a workshop including the Dyson 360 eye, a wirelessly controlled robotic vacuum cleaner.
The James Dyson Foundation would distribute the kit to state secondary schools free of charge, to inspire year 7-11 students and promote science, technology, engineering and mathematics careers. The proposed workshop comprises a challenge-based activity with a competitive aspect, where students would work in teams of 5-6.
Students would aim to complete four robot games themed challenges to earn table-tennis balls, allowing them extra shots at the final basket challenge.
The Dyson 360 eye robot and attachments would be programmed to solve challenges via a tablet, using graphical programming language Google Blockly.
My role was the design development of a robot arm, gripper attachment and ball launcher which fit into the robot alongside a base and cover.
The grippers were made from as few lasercut pieced of MDF as possible.
The launcher's dimensions needed to be very specific to allow the trigger to function correctly.
The robot arm needed to be sturdy but also easy to assemble.

The final designs intend to be fun, interesting and easy to assemble, while permitting all bespoke components to be manufactured from low-volume production techniques using Dyson’s existing facilities.
The final prototype has a friendly appearance and impressive performance given exclusive use of lasercut MDF and 3D printed parts, costing £61 including actuators.
The head of computing at Beechen Cliff Highschool in Bath stated she would be “very excited” to use the product in her lessons.
To enrich the project, I learned CSS and HTML from online tutorials to allow me to produce a demonstration website for the scheme.
